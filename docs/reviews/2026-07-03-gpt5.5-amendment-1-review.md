Four blocking findings, all confirmed and incorporated into the revised
Amendment 1:

1. **P10 overclaimed manifest-authority independence** — the manifest
   self-signature proves possession, not authority (an attacker's invented

## Full review text (verbatim)

Yes. My judgment: **Amendment 1 is a real improvement and should exist**,
but I would **not pre-register it exactly as written**. It closes a serious
weakness in the original H0 gate: the original required a machine-checked
model and proof before Phase 1, but left too much room for the proof target
to drift toward what was convenient to model. The amendment’s decision to
pre-register theorem statements, adversary capabilities, tooling rationale,
manifest-authority semantics, temporal-anchor semantics, and a
falsification-style agreement gate is methodologically strong.

The blocking issues I would fix first are: **P10 overclaims
manifest-authority independence**, **P5’s temporal condition is one-sided
despite saying “within δ”**, **`VALID_DEGRADED` needs a stricter policy
lattice**, and **“Tamarin or ProVerif” still leaves room for tool-shopping
unless the choice rule is pre-registered**.

## Overall assessment

The amendment strengthens the pre-registration in exactly the right
direction. The original H0 already had the right instinct: distinguish the
model from the code, distinguish symbolic proof from computational
assumptions, require an informal written proof, and refuse to proceed to
Phase 1 if the foundation does not hold. The amendment makes that gate much
harder to pass accidentally by fixing the proof target in advance: P1–P10
name the actual properties; A1.3 names the adversary; A1.4 assigns tools to
proof obligations; and A1.7 turns the agreement gate into a counterexample
hunt rather than a confirmation ritual.

That is the right amendment for a project whose whole thesis is “declare
what you cannot see.” It also preserves the original’s ranked-objective
discipline: this is still primarily a demonstration of building,
validating, observing, securing, and deploying an attestation service, with
commercial service treated as upside rather than premise.

## What is strongest

The strongest part is **A1.0/A1.2**. The phrase “a proof that does not
protect the project” identifies the right failure mode. Formal verification
theater often happens when the proof obligation becomes “whatever our model
proved,” not “the security claim we needed.” The amendment’s property list
gives H0 a fixed referent before Band 0 starts.

The second strongest part is **A1.7**. Replacing “author and AI agree” with
“non-author models try to construct counterexamples” is a material
improvement. It aligns with the original hard rule that adversarial tests
are authored by a different model, and it also narrows the original
model-to-code gap by requiring model-derived conformance vectors for H1a.

The third strong move is **A1.6’s correction of timestamp semantics**.
OpenTimestamps itself describes a timestamp as proving that data existed
prior to some point in time, not as proving exact creation time or
authority; so the amendment is right to say the anchor is an upper bound on
creation time and not a full “issued at exactly t” oracle.

## Blocking issue 1: P10 overclaims manifest-authority independence

A1.5 correctly spots the circularity in the original manifest-authority
story: anchoring a manifest proves existence, not authority, and a manifest
signed only by the keys it authorizes is circular. That diagnosis is
excellent.

The proposed cure is not quite as strong as the text claims. The three
evidences are:

1. DNSSEC chain snapshot for `wamason.com`;
2. anchored repository publication;
3. manifest self-signature by the issuer keys.

The self-signature should **not** be counted as an independent authority
channel. It proves possession/control of the private key corresponding to
the manifest key, and it binds the manifest bytes to that key. But an
attacker inventing an unauthorized key can always self-sign with that key.
So the real authority claim is not “N = 3 independent evidences of
authority”; it is closer to “two external publication/authority channels
plus proof-of-possession.”

That matters because P10 currently says an adversary controlling fewer than
N of the N channels cannot cause a non-authorized key to satisfy
`VALID_STRICT`. If self-signature is counted as one of N, then the
adversary effectively gets one channel for free when substituting their own
key. The property can still be made true, but the model should express the
actual threshold honestly.

I would revise A1.5/P10 along these lines:

> The manifest carries two independent archived authority-publication
> evidences, DNSSEC and anchored repository publication, plus a manifest
> self-signature that proves possession and binds the manifest bytes to the
> asserted issuer keys. The self-signature is not authority evidence by
> itself and is never counted as an independent authority channel.
> `VALID_STRICT` requires all external authority-publication evidences plus
> proof-of-possession. `VALID_DEGRADED` may accept fewer external evidences
> only under an explicit policy naming which authority evidence was waived
> and why.

A stronger version would add a genuinely third external evidence channel.
But if the real design is DNSSEC + repository + self-signature, the
amendment should say that plainly.

## Blocking issue 2: P5 says “within δ” but formalizes only one side

A1.6 says the verifier accepts a claimed issue time only if the anchor
confirms **within δ** of it, with a strict default of δ = 72 hours. But
P5’s stated property is one-sided: “no accepted receipt whose anchor time
exceeds its claimed issue time by more than δ.”

That prevents excessive backdating, but it does not forbid an anchor that
is far **before** the claimed issue time. Maybe that is acceptable, but
then “within δ” is not the right wording. If the receipt claims “issued at
time t,” I think strict verification should require something like:

```text
claimed_issue_time - ε <= anchor_time <= claimed_issue_time + δ
```

where `ε` is whatever tolerance you allow for Bitcoin timestamp skew and
clock/reporting ambiguity. Bitcoin block times are not exact wall-clock
timestamps; Bitcoin’s developer reference says block time must be greater
than the median time of the previous 11 blocks and not more than two hours
in the future according to the validating node’s clock.

A cleaner alternative is to avoid pretending the anchor proves “issue time”
at all. Split the receipt semantics into:

```text
declared_issue_time: what the issuer claims
anchor_not_after_time: what OTS/Bitcoin establishes
strict_temporal_policy: anchor_not_after_time must be no later than declared_issue_time + δ,
                        and declared_issue_time must not be materially after anchor_not_after_time
                        unless explicitly allowed by policy
```

The key point: P5 should either become genuinely two-sided, or the prose
should stop saying “within δ.”

## Blocking issue 3: `VALID_DEGRADED` needs a policy lattice, not just an escape hatch

The amendment uses `VALID_DEGRADED` carefully, but not yet rigorously
enough. P2 allows a package with fewer signatures than its manifest
requires to become `VALID_DEGRADED` under explicit verifier policy, while
P4 says a required check that fails yields `INVALID` and a check that
cannot be performed yields `UNVERIFIABLE`, never a valid state by fallback.

That can be consistent, but only if “policy” is formalized. Otherwise
`VALID_DEGRADED` risks becoming the universal waiver that the whole
verifier-state design was meant to prevent.

I would add a new subsection, perhaps A1.2.1:

> **Non-waivable checks.** No verifier policy may waive canonical-byte
> integrity, type/domain separation, boundary framing, key-to-issuer
> binding for accepted signatures, or temporal-anchor consistency. Degraded
> policies may only weaken declared redundancy requirements, such as
> accepting a subset of issue-time signatures, accepting fewer
> manifest-authority evidences, or accepting an algorithm whose trust root
> is no longer independently recoverable. Every degraded verdict records
> the precise waived check set.

Then define the relation:

```text
strict_policy ⊑ degraded_policy
```

and require monotonicity:

```text
If a package is INVALID under a non-waivable check,
no degraded policy may turn it into VALID_DEGRADED.
```

That keeps `VALID_DEGRADED` useful for long-term verification without
letting it become a fail-open path. It also matches the original motivation
for typed verifier states: avoid collapsing “failed,” “unverifiable,” and
“valid under weaker assumptions” into a boolean.

## Blocking issue 4: “Tamarin or ProVerif” needs a pre-registered choice rule

A1.4’s tool split is directionally right. TLA+ is a natural fit for
state-machine and temporal properties; Tamarin and ProVerif are natural
fits for active symbolic adversaries. Tamarin is explicitly positioned as a
symbolic protocol tool supporting falsification and unbounded verification,
and ProVerif is an automated symbolic protocol verifier used in the
Dolev-Yao model.

But “Tamarin or ProVerif” still leaves a subtle drift vector: you can try
both and keep the one that makes the proof easiest. That is not necessarily
dishonest, but for a pre-registration it should be governed.

I would add:

> Tool choice is fixed before theorem-proving begins. A bounded spike may
> compare Tamarin and ProVerif only on expressiveness/readability for
> P1–P3/P7 under A1.3, not on which tool proves the desired result. The
> choice, rejected alternative, and reason are committed before substantive
> proof work. Switching tools later requires a signed amendment explaining
> the blocker.

This preserves flexibility while preventing tool-shopping.

## Important issue: P3 should name the concrete Ed25519 verification profile

P3 is right to include key-substitution / DSKS-style attacks in the
adversary model. Signature protocols can fail when a signature is treated
as proving “this key uniquely owns this message,” rather than merely “this
verification equation holds under this public key.” DSKS/key-substitution
attacks are a known class of signature-protocol pitfalls.

The amendment says real Ed25519 does not provide the needed binding “by
default,” then splits discharge between model-level identity binding and an
implementation assumption about hardened verification. I would make that
more operational and less arguable. RFC 8032 defines EdDSA/Ed25519 and
includes implementation/test-vector material, but real deployments still
need to choose strict verification behavior around canonical encodings,
low-order points, cofactor behavior, and library compatibility.

Recommended addition:

> The implementation verification profile is part of H1a evidence: exact
> library/version, accepted signature encodings, public-key validation
> behavior, low-order point handling, cofactor equation variant, and
> negative test vectors are recorded. Verification input includes domain
> separation, algorithm identifier, issuer identity, key fingerprint,
> manifest hash, canonicalization version, and object type inside the
> signed bytes.

That turns P3 from “we assume a hardened variant” into something testable.

## Important issue: P8 needs rejection semantics, not only golden vectors

The original’s choice to bind canonicalization to RFC 8785/JCS is sensible:
RFC 8785 defines deterministic JSON canonicalization for cryptographic uses
by constraining JSON to I-JSON, using ECMAScript primitive serialization,
and deterministic property sorting. The original already recognizes the
sharp edge around numbers and says non-exact-double values must be
string-encoded.

For P8, I would add explicit **negative-domain** rules. Golden vectors show
what valid encodings produce; they do not fully specify what invalid or
ambiguous inputs must do.

Add rejection tests for at least:

```text
duplicate object names
NaN / Infinity
integers outside exactly representable range
-0 / numeric edge cases
non-I-JSON strings
unexpected Unicode normalization assumptions
unknown canonicalization versions
extra trailing bytes
type-tag mismatch
length-prefix mismatch
```

The direct proof of injectivity should be over the accepted payload domain,
and the verifier should reject anything outside that domain rather than
canonicalizing it “helpfully.”

## Important issue: A1.1 makes H0 iterative, but “no model can be built” is hard to falsify

A1.1 is right that modeling is iterative; a failed first proof attempt
should not kill the project. But “H0 is falsified when no model of the
construction can be built” may be too hard to operationalize. In practice,
you rarely prove “no model can be built”; you decide that the construction
must be weakened, the property must be withdrawn, or the model no longer
corresponds to the intended system.

I would revise the falsifier to something more auditable:

> H0 is falsified if any A1.2 property cannot be discharged without either
> weakening the property, moving it from `[model]`/`[proof]` to
> `[assumption]`, excluding a declared A1.3 adversary capability, or
> changing the construction so materially that it no longer implements the
> original dual-signature + temporal-anchor design. Any such weakening
> requires a new amendment; absent that amendment, Phase 1 may not begin.

That gives future readers a concrete way to see whether Band 0 succeeded,
failed, or changed scope.

## Smaller wording and consistency fixes

A1.8 says the hard invariants and hypotheses are untouched, but the
amendment does add new H1a obligations through model-derived conformance
vectors and adds new H0 review requirements through the falsification task.
I would say: “The original objectives and invariants are not weakened; this
amendment adds resolution/evidence obligations.”

For revocation, clarify whether authorization is evaluated at
`claimed_issue_time`, `anchor_time`, or an interval. The original says
revocation after anchored issue time does not retroactively invalidate a
receipt, while revocation at or before issue time yields `INVALID`. Once
A1.6 distinguishes claimed issue time from anchor upper-bound semantics, P6
should use the same time vocabulary.

For wrappers, P7 is good, but I would explicitly require domain-separation
tags inside signed bytes for: base receipt, wrapper receipt, manifest,
authority evidence, conformance vector, and review-recency attestation. The
original wrapper semantics are careful that wrapping commits to inner
bytes, not to the inner verification result; P7 should preserve that
distinction exactly.

For A1.7, specify what counts as “non-author models”: model/vendor/date,
prompt, temperature/configuration where available, exact input bundle hash,
and whether any reviewer had access to previous review transcripts. That
makes the falsification review itself reproducible enough to be credible.

## My recommended pre-registration edits before anchoring

I would make these four changes before signing/anchoring Amendment 1:

1. Replace P10/A1.5’s “N = 3 independent authority evidences” with
   “external authority-publication evidences plus proof-of-possession,”
   unless you add a real third independent authority channel.

2. Rewrite P5 so the temporal policy is either genuinely two-sided or
   explicitly one-sided. Do not say “within δ” while proving only
   `anchor_time <= claimed_issue_time + δ`.

3. Add a short “degraded policy semantics” section with non-waivable checks
   and monotonicity rules.

4. Add a tool-choice discipline for Tamarin vs. ProVerif before substantive
   proof work begins.

With those changes, I would regard the amendment as substantially stronger
than the original H0 text and appropriate to pre-register. As drafted, it
is very good, but P10 and P5 in particular are exactly the kind of subtle
overclaim a hostile reviewer would seize on.
