   right kind of control: it defines non-waivable checks, limits

4. **Tool-shopping is now controlled.** A1.4’s bounded spike and committed

> **This amendment now does what it is supposed to do: it makes Band 0
> meaningfully hard to pass by accident.**

It also makes the project stronger as a legitimacy artifact. The provenance

## The remaining material issue: P6 should not use only `declared_issue_time`

This is the one place where I think the revised draft introduces a subtle
new weakness.

P6 currently says:

> Key authorization is evaluated at `declared_issue_time`, as bounded by
> the anchor per P5.

That sounds clean, but it creates a revocation/backdating interaction.

The anchor proves only that the bytes existed **not after** `anchor_time`.
It does not prove that they existed at `declared_issue_time`. P5 allows
`anchor_time` to be up to δ after `declared_issue_time`; with the current
strict default, that means up to 72 hours.

So consider this case:

```text
T0: key is authorized
T1: key is revoked
T2: attacker or compromised path signs bytes after revocation
T3: bytes are anchored
```

If the attacker sets:

```text
declared_issue_time = T1 - small_margin
anchor_time         = T2/T3, still within δ
```

then P5 can pass, and P6 evaluates authorization before revocation. The
receipt can appear valid even though the actual signing may have occurred
after revocation.

That is not a theoretical nit. It is exactly the kind of ambiguity created
by the fact that the anchor is only an upper bound. P5 bounds backdating to
δ, but P6 currently lets that bounded backdating matter for authorization.

I would revise P6 to be conservative:

> **P6 — Revocation time-relativity.** Key authorization is evaluated over
> the temporal uncertainty window induced by P5. For `VALID_STRICT`, the
> key must be authorized through `anchor_time` — or, more explicitly, no
> revocation effective at or before `anchor_time` may invalidate the key.
> Revocation effective after `anchor_time` does not retroactively change
> the verdict. Revocation effective at or before `anchor_time` yields
> `INVALID`, unless a future amendment introduces independent,
> service-independent evidence proving the signature existed before
> revocation.

A slightly more formal version:

```text
VALID_STRICT requires:
  key_authorized(declared_issue_time)
  ∧ no_revocation_effective_at_or_before(anchor_time)
```

Or, if you want to express the uncertainty interval:

```text
VALID_STRICT requires key authorization throughout:
  [declared_issue_time, anchor_time]
```

That is stricter than evaluating only at `declared_issue_time`, but it
preserves the service’s evidentiary posture. It says: because we cannot
prove the exact signing time, strict verification refuses to accept
receipts whose authorization status changed before the anchor confirmed.

This may cause some honest receipts to fail if a key is revoked between
declaration and anchor confirmation. But that is the right fail-closed
behavior. The issuance protocol already says final issuance is not complete
until anchor confirmation. If revocation happens before confirmation, the
system cannot honestly prove the receipt was safely issued before
revocation.

## Related P5 wording issue: “defeats” should probably be “bounds”

P5 says the δ side defeats backdating and the ε side defeats post-dating. I
would soften that to “bounds.”

The construction does not eliminate backdating or post-dating inside the
tolerance window. It bounds them.

Suggested wording:

> The δ side bounds backdating: a forger signing new bytes cannot obtain a
> conforming anchor for a claimed time more than δ in the past. The ε side
> bounds post-dating: an issuer cannot declare an issue time more than ε
> after the anchor and later present the receipt as fresh.

That is more precise and avoids a hostile reviewer saying, “No, you still
allow up to δ of backdating and ε of post-dating.”

## Clarify that δ/ε are not receipt-controlled

P5 says δ and ε are “declared verifier-policy parameters recorded per
receipt.” That is close, but I would be careful. If the receipt itself can
enlarge δ or ε, then a malicious or compromised issuer can weaken temporal
soundness by writing a huge tolerance into the signed bytes.

I would separate three things:

```text
observed_anchor_delay = anchor_time - declared_issue_time
receipt_policy_version = the issue-time policy identifier
verifier_policy = the policy chosen by the verifier
```

Then say:

> A receipt may record the issue-time policy version and observed anchor
> delay, but it may not choose its own strict temporal tolerances. The
> reference verifier’s strict policy fixes maximum δ and ε. A verifier may
> choose stricter bounds; no degraded policy may enlarge δ or ε beyond the
> strict maxima without a new signed amendment.

That preserves “verifier policy lives with the verifier,” which was one of
the original design strengths.

## A1.7 has one overclaim: conformance vectors do not “prove” code conformance

This line is too strong:

> model-derived vectors prove conformance to the model

They do not. They provide implementation evidence against model-derived
fixtures. That is valuable, but it is still testing.

I would change it to:

> model-derived vectors evidence conformance to the model on extracted
> traces; the adversarially-authored red-bar attacks hunt for what is
> outside it.

Or:

> model-derived vectors test the implementation against the model’s
> concrete predictions; they do not prove full implementation/model
> equivalence.

This matters because the original pre-registration is careful that the
model-to-code gap is attested/tested, not proven. The amendment should
preserve that discipline.

## A1.2.1 should explicitly handle “cannot evaluate” for non-waivable checks

A1.2.1 says a package failing a non-waivable check is `INVALID` under every
policy. That is right for failed checks. But for a non-waivable check that
cannot be evaluated, the result should remain `UNVERIFIABLE`, not
`INVALID`, and still must not be promoted.

I would add one sentence:

> If a non-waivable check cannot be performed, the result is
> `UNVERIFIABLE`, never `VALID_DEGRADED`; if it is performed and fails, the
> result is `INVALID`.

That keeps A1.2.1 aligned with P4’s partition.

## A1.5/P10 are now much better, with one small archival clarification

The manifest-authority section is now honest and much stronger. The
residual-risk disclosure is especially good: it admits correlated control
and trust-anchor recursion rather than pretending plural evidence magically
creates root authority.

The only wording improvement I would make is in A1.5 item 2. DNSSEC
evidence is explicitly archived in the bundle; repository evidence should
be stated the same way.

Suggested wording:

> **Anchored repository publication** — the public git commit publishing
> the same fingerprints, including the commit object, signature, relevant
> history/proof material, and OTS proof, archived in the bundle.

That prevents a future reader from wondering whether verification depends
on live GitHub/GitLab availability.

## P3 is now much better; one wording tweak

The Ed25519/key-binding section is much improved. The verification profile
obligation is exactly the right move.

I would tweak:

> real Ed25519 does not provide it by default

to:

> Ed25519 verification alone does not provide protocol-level issuer binding
> or exclusive ownership.

That is more precise. The issue is not that Ed25519 is broken; it is that a
signature verification equation is not, by itself, the whole issuer-binding
protocol.

## Provenance wording: avoid overstating “external”

The provenance section is useful, but “external” can be read as independent
human/external organization. To avoid that, I would say:

> GPT-5.5, non-author AI reviewer external to the authoring session

or:

> non-author AI reviewer

That keeps the evidentiary value without making the review sound more
independent than it is.

Also, because this is still a draft, make sure this sentence is true at
anchoring time:

> Its full text and the prompts used are committed as a review artifact.

If the artifact is not already committed, change to:

> will be committed before this amendment is signed and anchored

until it is actually true.

## My recommended edits before anchoring

I would make these edits before pre-registering:

1. **Revise P6 to evaluate authorization through `anchor_time`, not only
   `declared_issue_time`.** This is the only material issue I would treat
   as blocking.

2. **Change P5’s “defeats” language to “bounds.”**

3. **Clarify that δ/ε are verifier-policy maxima, not receipt-controlled
   escape hatches.**

4. **Change A1.7’s “prove conformance to the model” to “evidence/test
   conformance against model-derived traces.”**

5. **Add the P4/A1.2.1 clarification: failed non-waivable check →
   `INVALID`; unevaluable non-waivable check → `UNVERIFIABLE`; neither may
   become `VALID_DEGRADED`.**

6. **Clarify that anchored repository evidence is archived in the bundle.**

With those changes, I would be comfortable saying this amendment is ready
to sign and anchor. It is now a strong legitimacy artifact: not because it
claims perfection, but because it repeatedly turns discovered ambiguity
into dated, explicit obligations.
